Section 3~19%

Threat Hunting

Proactively search for anomalies and indicators of compromise the detections may have missed.

Hunting techniques

Run hypothesis-driven UDM searches across logs, analyse user behaviour for anomalies, and pivot on IOCs (hash/IP/URL/domain) via GTI. Use retrohunt to apply a new rule or fresh indicators to historical data — the way you discover earlier compromise.

⚠️ Exam Trap: When you must apply brand-new indicators or a new rule to past data to find prior compromise, that's a retrohunt.

Risk-driven hunting

Use the entity risk score to surface anomalous users/assets, and SCC toxic combinations to see when several individually-minor issues chain into a real attack path to sensitive data.

Frameworks & hypothesis-driven hunting

Hunting isn't aimless log-staring — it's structured. Anchor hunts to a framework so coverage is measurable and repeatable:

  • MITRE ATT&CK — map hunts to tactics/techniques (e.g., T1078 Valid Accounts) to find blind spots in your detection coverage.
  • Pyramid of Pain — hunting on hashes/IPs is cheap for the adversary to change; hunting on TTPs hurts them most. Aim up the pyramid.
  • Hypothesis → UDM search → pivot — state what you expect an attacker to do, query for it, then pivot across entities and indicators on the results.
UP THE PYRAMID Hashes and IPs are trivial to swap; TTPs are the costliest for an attacker to change — that's where durable hunts live.

UDM search craft & threat intel pivots

The hunter's primary instrument is UDM search over normalised events. Strong hunts: filter by UDM field (not raw text), aggregate to spot outliers (rare process, rare parent-child pair, first-seen domain), and pivot on the survivors. Lean on Applied Threat Intelligence and GTI so curated, prioritised IoCs auto-match your telemetry rather than you pasting indicators by hand.

  • Statistical/rare-event hunting — surface the unusual (rare child processes, low-prevalence binaries) rather than known-bad only.
  • IoC enrichment — pivot a suspicious hash/IP/domain through GTI for actor, campaign, and prevalence context.
  • Entity risk score — let aggregated risk float anomalous users/assets to the top of the hunt queue.
⚠️ Exam Trap: "Find prior compromise from a brand-new indicator" is a retrohunt (rule/indicator over historical data). "Proactively look for the unknown" is hunting via UDM search. Don't confuse the two.

🧠 Warm-Up Quiz

Q1. A new advisory lists malicious hashes/IPs; check if they appeared in the last 6 months of logs. What do you run?

Reveal answer

A retrohunt against historical data.

Applies indicators to past events to reveal earlier compromise.

Q2. Several low-severity SCC findings chain into a path to sensitive data. Which concept surfaces this?

Reveal answer

Toxic combinations (attack path).

SCC chains issues into an exploitable path.

Q3. You want hunts that are hardest for an adversary to evade. Which layer of the Pyramid of Pain do you target?

Reveal answer

TTPs (tactics, techniques, procedures).

Hashes/IPs are trivial to swap; changing TTPs is far costlier for the attacker.

Q4. You want curated, prioritised IoCs to automatically match against your telemetry instead of pasting them manually. What provides this?

Reveal answer

Applied Threat Intelligence (with GTI).

Auto-matches prioritised curated indicators against your events.

📝 Exam-Style Practice

Q1. Intel says an actor is active in your sector but gives no concrete indicators — only that they abuse valid accounts and living-off-the-land binaries. You have one day to hunt productively. What is the most effective approach?

  • A) Wait for the intel team to publish hashes and IPs, then retrohunt those across historical logs
  • B) Form ATT&CK-mapped hypotheses for the named techniques and run UDM searches for rare process behaviour
  • C) Block all the actor's known domains at the firewall and monitor for renewed connection attempts
  • D) Raise the severity of every existing detection so more of the actor's activity gets escalated
Reveal answer & explanation

Correct: B. With only TTPs to go on, hypothesis-driven hunting mapped to ATT&CK techniques (valid-account abuse, LOLBins) and executed as UDM searches for rare behaviour is exactly the high-pyramid hunt the situation calls for.

A: Waiting for hashes/IPs forfeits the day and pins you to the most evadable, lowest-pyramid indicators. C: You were given no domains to block, and blocking is a control action, not a hunt for present compromise. D: Cranking severity on existing rules creates alert noise without searching for the unknown activity these detections were never built to catch.

Q2. A threat advisory drops at 2pm with 30 fresh file hashes tied to a new loader. Leadership asks whether any of these touched the estate over the past quarter. What do you do in SecOps?

  • A) Add the hashes to a reference list and wait for live detections to fire on future matches
  • B) Open an interactive UDM search and manually scroll today's process events for the hashes
  • C) Run a retrohunt with the hashes against the last quarter of historical telemetry
  • D) Build a Looker Studio dashboard that trends hash prevalence across the quarter
Reveal answer & explanation

Correct: C. Applying brand-new indicators to past data to discover earlier compromise is the definition of a retrohunt, and it answers the "did this touch us in the last quarter" question directly.

A: A reference list plus live detection only catches future matches, ignoring the historical question asked. B: Manually scrolling today's events neither covers the quarter nor scales to 30 hashes. D: A dashboard visualises trends but does not search history for specific indicators to confirm or rule out prior compromise.

Q3. A hunter notices a service account suddenly authenticating from a new country, then enumerating storage buckets it never touched before. They want the platform to keep surfacing accounts behaving this anomalously without writing a bespoke rule for each pattern. What should they lean on?

  • A) The entity risk score, which aggregates anomalous signals to float high-risk users and assets up
  • B) A single high-severity YARA-L rule that fires on any authentication from a new country
  • C) SCC Security Health Analytics to flag the bucket configuration as a misconfiguration finding
  • D) A SOAR playbook that disables any account seen authenticating outside its usual region
Reveal answer & explanation

Correct: A. The entity risk score aggregates multiple anomalous behaviours into a single prioritised signal, surfacing risky accounts for hunting without a hand-written rule per pattern.

B: A blunt new-country rule generates heavy false positives and only captures one of the two suspicious behaviours. C: SHA checks bucket posture/configuration, not anomalous account behaviour over time. D: Auto-disabling on geography is a heavy-handed response action that would lock out legitimate travel and isn't a hunting mechanism.
