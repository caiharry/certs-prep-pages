Section 5: Supporting Compliance

Map regulatory and industry requirements onto Google Cloud controls (~11%). The smallest slice on the paper, and mostly common sense once you know the shared responsibility model — easy marks if you don't fumble three look-alike services.

Section 5 🟩 ~11%

Only one in nine questions lives here, so don't over-invest — but don't skip it either, because the marks are cheap. The whole domain boils down to: know who's responsible for what, keep data in the right region, control Google's own access, and prove compliance with evidence. The one trap is three services that sound identical (Transparency, Approval, Assured Workloads) but do very different jobs. 📜✅

5.1 The Shared Responsibility Model

Compliance starts with knowing where Google's job ends and yours begins. Google secures the infrastructure (hardware, the global network, the hypervisor, physical data centres). You secure what you put on it: identity, IAM configuration, your data, and your application code. The split shifts as you move up the stack — with serverless products Google takes on more, but your data and access decisions are always yours.

🤝 "Shared Fate", Not Just Shared Responsibility

Google's modern framing is shared fate: Google doesn't just draw a line and walk away — it provides secure-by-default blueprints, Assured Workloads, and risk-protection programs to help you hold up your side. But the accountability for configuration and data never transfers.

5.2 The Three Look-Alikes: Transparency vs Approval vs Assured Workloads

This is the only genuinely tricky bit of the domain. All three concern regulated estates and Google's access to your data — but they sit at different strengths.

Control What It Does Strength
Access Transparency Logs when Google staff access your content (e.g. during a support case). Visibility only — you SEE it, after the fact.
Access Approval Google must request, and you must explicitly approve, before that access happens. A GATE — you approve it first.
Assured Workloads A compliance-boundary folder enforcing data residency, personnel controls, and an approved-service set. A BOUNDARY — broad enforcement for FedRAMP, CJIS, EU sovereignty, etc.

💡 SEE / GATE / BOUND

Transparency lets you SEE (a passive log). Approval is the GATE (you say yes first). Assured Workloads draws the BOUNDary (residency + personnel + services). Match the verb in the stem to the right control.

"We need a log of Google access" = Access Transparency. "We must approve it first" = Access Approval. Don't confuse the passive log with the active gate. And if the stem adds "support staff must be in-country" or "only approved services / specific region," that personnel-and-residency bundle is Assured Workloads — the others can't enforce personnel location.

5.3 Enforcing Data Residency

Two tools enforce where data and resources live, and you should know when each fits:

Tool What It Enforces Use When...
Resource-location org policy (gcp.resourceLocations) Blocks resource creation outside approved regions; inherits to all current & future projects. You just need a residency guardrail without a full compliance program.
Assured Workloads Residency plus personnel controls, approved services, and regime alignment. A named regime (FedRAMP, CJIS, EU sovereignty) demands the full bundle.
Residency is preventive, not detective. If the requirement is "must never be created outside region X, across all current and future projects, with no per-project setup," the answer is the resource-location org policy at the org node — not tagging-and-auditing (detective, after the fact) and not a firewall policy (governs traffic, not where resources are provisioned).

5.4 Demonstrating Compliance With Evidence

Mapping a requirement (SOC 2, PCI DSS, ISO 27001, data sovereignty) onto Google Cloud means translating it into concrete controls and being able to prove them:

  • Security Command Center compliance dashboard — maps Security Health Analytics findings to benchmarks like CIS, PCI DSS, and NIST out of the box. Continuous checks, exportable evidence, minimal build.
  • Cloud Audit Logs — the who-did-what record auditors ask for.
  • CMEK — demonstrable key control for data-at-rest requirements.
  • Scoping — decide which projects are in scope for each framework and isolate them with the resource hierarchy and VPC Service Controls, so the audit surface stays small.

💡 Don't Re-Build the Benchmark

If the stem says "continuously checked against an industry benchmark, with the least custom build," the answer is the SCC compliance dashboard — never "export logs to BigQuery and write SQL to re-implement PCI." Re-implementing the benchmark by hand is exactly the wrong answer.

"The auditor asked for evidence of continuous compliance. I showed them the SCC dashboard. They asked for a second espresso instead — we were done early." ☕📊

5.5 Named Frameworks: What GCP Controls Map To

The exam expects you to name which GCP control satisfies which regulatory regime. You don't need to memorise the full standard — just the GCP handle for each requirement and the gaps that trip people up.

Framework What it demands Key GCP controls Watch out for
FedRAMP US federal cloud security baseline (High / Moderate) Assured Workloads FedRAMP High/Moderate folder, gcp.resourceLocations (US regions only), ITAR if applicable FedRAMP + ITAR together require personnel controls — only Assured Workloads bundles all three
HIPAA Protect PHI (Protected Health Information) Google BAA, CMEK for data-at-rest, VPC-SC perimeter, Data Access audit logs Not all GCP services are HIPAA-eligible — you must check Google's HIPAA implementation guide and use only covered services
PCI DSS Isolate and audit cardholder data Assured Workloads or VPC-SC perimeter, Data Access logs, CMEK, Cloud DLP tokenisation for card numbers Tokenise card numbers with Cloud DLP before storage; raw PANs must never appear in logs
ISO 27001 / SOC 2 Demonstrate information security management Inherit Google's infrastructure certifications; supplement with your own IAM controls, VPC-SC, audit logs Google's cert covers the infra — you must certify your configuration separately
GDPR EU data residency; right to erasure; PII protection Assured Workloads EU or gcp.resourceLocations (EU regions), CMEK kill-switch for right-to-erasure, Cloud DLP for PII discovery CMEK key destruction satisfies cryptographic erasure — you can't physically delete distributed data, so key-destruction is the accepted substitute

🏛️ Inherited vs Additive Compliance

Google holds certifications for ISO 27001, SOC 1/2/3, FedRAMP, and more. You inherit those for the Google-managed infrastructure layer (data centres, global network, hypervisor). But auditors also assess your layer — IAM configuration, key management, network controls, audit logging. Google's cert is a necessary baseline, not a get-out-of-audit-free card. Both halves must be in place.

HIPAA does NOT mean all GCP services are automatically HIPAA-eligible. Google signs a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) covering a defined list of services. Services outside that list must not be used to process PHI — even if they're otherwise secure. Always consult the Google Cloud HIPAA implementation guide and restrict your architecture to covered services.
"Our HIPAA audit went perfectly — we'd stored all the PHI in a covered service. Unfortunately, the cover was a sticky note labelled 'do not peek.' Reader, they peeked." 🩺📋

5.6 Risk Manager & Policy Intelligence

This set of tools helps you understand your IAM posture, debug access decisions, and test policy changes safely. They're often tested in scenarios where you need to audit who has access to what, or troubleshoot why a service account was denied.

Risk Manager

Risk Manager exports a snapshot of your organisation's security posture as a structured risk report. Its primary audience is cyber-insurance underwriters — it tells them how well-controlled your GCP estate is and can influence premiums. It doesn't enforce anything; it describes.

Policy Intelligence Suite

  • Policy Analyzer — asks "who can do what on resource X?" across the entire org. Runs a broad IAM query and returns all principals that have a given permission on a specified resource, including inherited grants from parent nodes.
  • Policy Troubleshooter — asks "why was this specific request denied right now?" Given a principal, a resource, and a permission, it walks the IAM bindings and org policy constraints to explain the denial. Point-in-time debug mode.
  • Policy Simulator — runs a hypothetical: "what would happen if I applied this new policy?" It evaluates the proposed change against recent access logs to show which accesses would be allowed or denied. Read-only and hypothetical — it never applies the policy.
  • Active Assist recommendations — continuous suggestions across cost, security, and manageability. For IAM this surfaces over-privileged roles, unused service accounts, and publicly exposed resources.

💡 Analyzer vs Troubleshooter — WHO vs WHY

Policy Analyzer = WHO has access to resource X (broad org-wide query). Use it when you want a full map of principals.
Policy Troubleshooter = WHY was I denied right now (point-in-time debug). Use it when a specific user or service account hit a 403.

Policy Simulator is hypothetical and read-only. It evaluates what a proposed IAM policy would do by replaying recent access logs against the new policy. It does not apply the policy change, and it does not reflect real-time current state — it uses a window of recent access history. If the stem says "test a role binding change before applying it," Simulator is the answer. If the stem says "apply and roll back," that's a different workflow entirely.

🛡️ When to Pull Which Tool

  • Pre-change safety check → Policy Simulator (hypothetical replay against recent logs)
  • "Who has access to this bucket across the org?" → Policy Analyzer
  • "Why did service account X get a 403 on dataset Y just now?" → Policy Troubleshooter
  • "Show our cyber-insurer how secure we are" → Risk Manager
  • "Fix my over-permissioned roles automatically" → Active Assist IAM recommendations
"Used Policy Troubleshooter to find out why the service account was denied. Turns out the IAM binding was on the wrong project. The service account, meanwhile, had been filing a formal grievance for three sprints." 📨😤

5.7 Automating Compliance Posture

A static compliance check is only a compliance snapshot. Production environments drift. The exam tests whether you can wire up an automated posture that detects and fixes drift without a human in the loop for every finding.

The Posture Stack

  • Org Policy constraints — preventive guardrails set at the org or folder level (e.g. require CMEK on all BigQuery datasets via constraints/gcp.resourceLocations and custom constraints, block unencrypted Cloud Storage buckets). Violations are blocked at resource creation — before SHA ever fires.
  • Security Health Analytics (SHA) in SCC — detective layer. Continuously scans your estate and produces findings when a resource violates a security best practice (public bucket, over-privileged IAM, no CMEK, etc.).
  • Assured Workloads — operates at the folder level to enforce a compliance regime end-to-end (residency + services + personnel). Works alongside Org Policy constraints, not instead of them.
  • SCC Posture templates — pre-built control sets for common compliance frameworks (PCI DSS, NIST 800-53, ISO 27001). Deploy as a baseline so you're not hand-crafting every org policy from scratch.
  • Custom org policy constraints — enforce business-specific rules beyond the built-in set: require a specific label on every resource, mandate CMEK on Cloud SQL, block public IP on GKE nodes. Written in Common Expression Language (CEL).

Automated Remediation Pattern

Detective findings are only useful if someone acts on them. The standard GCP automation pattern is:

Step Service What happens
1. Detect Security Command Center (SHA) SHA finds a public GCS bucket and creates a finding.
2. Notify Pub/Sub SCC exports the finding to a Pub/Sub topic in near-real time.
3. Trigger Cloud Functions (or Cloud Run) A function subscribed to the topic wakes up and reads the finding payload.
4. Remediate Cloud Storage / IAM API The function calls the API to remove the allUsers binding, making the bucket private again.
5. Confirm SCC / Audit Logs The finding is resolved; audit log records the programmatic change.

🔁 Automated Remediation in One Line

SCC finding → Pub/Sub notification → Cloud Function trigger → remediation API call. Memorise the chain. Questions usually describe it in a scenario and ask you to identify the missing component, or ask which service handles step N.

SHA findings show current state — they do NOT auto-remediate. If a bucket is accidentally made public and SHA fires, a finding appears in SCC. That's it. The bucket remains public until something acts on the finding. Auto-remediation only happens if you've wired the Pub/Sub pipeline yourself. "Enable Security Command Center" ≠ "enable auto-fix." Don't mix up the detective and the corrective layers.
"SHA found 47 public buckets. The team said 'great, SCC will fix them.' Reader: SCC had been watching in polite, documented horror for six months." 🪣😱

5.8 Audit Readiness & Evidence Collection Automation

Passing a security audit is not just about having the right controls — it is about proving they worked, consistently, over a period of time. The exam tests whether you can assemble an automated evidence collection pipeline rather than scrambling at audit time.

What Auditors Actually Ask For

Different log types answer different audit questions. Map the question to the log, not just to "Cloud Logging":

Audit Question Log / Service Notes
Who accessed what data? Data Access audit logs Must be explicitly enabled per service — disabled by default (except BigQuery which enables them automatically).
Who changed the configuration? Admin Activity audit logs Always enabled; records API calls that create, modify, or delete resources.
Who held which access at time T? IAM Recommender reports + Cloud Asset Inventory IAM policy history Recommender exports show granted vs. used permissions; Asset Inventory captures historical state.
How were encryption keys managed? Cloud KMS audit logs (Admin Activity + Data Access) Records every key version creation, rotation, destruction, and decrypt call.
What was the resource inventory at a point in time? Cloud Asset Inventory snapshots Export or query the asset feed to reconstruct the estate at any historical timestamp.

Audit Log Retention Strategy

Cloud Logging's default retention is insufficient for most compliance frameworks. Know the numbers and the fix:

  • _Required log bucket (Admin Activity + System Event logs): 400 days, fixed — Google mandates this and it cannot be changed.
  • _Default log bucket (Data Access, Access Transparency, user logs): 30 days default, configurable up to 3,650 days within Cloud Logging at extra cost.
  • Long-term retention (7 years for SOX/PCI DSS/HIPAA): create a log sink exporting all audit log buckets to a Cloud Storage bucket. Apply a Bucket Lock retention policy of 7 years. Cloud Storage is significantly cheaper for cold archival than extended Cloud Logging retention.
Default audit log retention is 30 days for the _Default bucket — for 7-year retention you need a log sink to Cloud Storage, not just enabling Data Access logs. Enabling Data Access logs makes the logs appear in Cloud Logging, but they still expire after 30 days if you don't sink them. A question describing "we enabled data access logging for PCI DSS but our auditor says we're missing logs from 14 months ago" has one root cause: no log sink to durable, locked Cloud Storage. Enabling the logs and retaining the logs are two separate actions.

Cloud Asset Inventory as the Evidence Snapshot Tool

Cloud Asset Inventory (CAI) records the complete configuration and IAM policy state of every resource in your organisation — and stores a history of how that state changed over time. This makes it the go-to tool for producing audit evidence of who had what access when.

  • Point-in-time snapshots: gcloud asset export dumps every resource and its attached IAM policy to a Cloud Storage file. Schedulable via Cloud Scheduler for daily snapshots submitted as audit evidence.
  • Temporal queries: gcloud asset get-history returns the full change history for a resource's IAM policy between two timestamps — directly answers "who had owner on project X during the SOX audit window?"
  • Policy analysis: analyzeIamPolicy finds all principals with a given permission on a resource (or transitively through group membership) — ideal for access-review reports.
  • Integration with SCC: CAI feeds resource metadata into Security Command Center, so SCC findings reference the actual resource configuration at detection time.

📋 CAI as the "Evidence Snapshot" Tool

When an auditor asks for an access review — a list of every principal with elevated permissions, what they had access to, and whether it was still needed — reach for Cloud Asset Inventory. Export IAM policy history for the audit window, cross-reference with IAM Recommender reports (which flag permissions granted but never used), and present the combination as evidence. CAI + Recommender = defensible access-review artefact.

Continuous Compliance Alerting: SCC → Pub/Sub → Notification Channels

Auditors increasingly want continuous compliance evidence, not just a year-end snapshot. The standard GCP alerting pipeline wires Security Command Center to human-readable channels:

  • SCC → Pub/Sub: configure SCC to export all findings (or filtered findings by severity/category) to a Pub/Sub topic in near-real time.
  • Pub/Sub → Cloud Functions / Workflows: subscribers can fan out findings to multiple destinations simultaneously — email, Slack, PagerDuty, or a ticketing system (Jira, ServiceNow).
  • SCC Notification Configs: a single SCC notification config publishes to one Pub/Sub topic; create multiple configs with different filters (e.g. one for CRITICAL findings, one for MEDIUM) routed to different topics and downstream consumers.
  • Evidence retention: persist raw finding payloads (JSON) from Pub/Sub to Cloud Storage or BigQuery for audit trail purposes — SCC itself retains findings for only a limited period at Standard tier.

💡 LAKE — The Audit Evidence Stack

Logs (Cloud Logging, sunk to Cloud Storage for 7 years). Asset snapshots (Cloud Asset Inventory for IAM history). Key management records (Cloud KMS audit logs). Events (SCC findings → Pub/Sub → notification channels). LAKE: if you can answer every audit question from these four sources, you're audit-ready. If one is missing, you'll be scrambling with partial evidence when the auditor arrives.

Enabling Data Access Audit Logs: The Easy Step You Cannot Skip

Admin Activity audit logs are always on. Data Access audit logs — which capture individual read/write operations on data — are off by default for most services (BigQuery is the exception: DATA_READ and DATA_WRITE are enabled automatically). You must explicitly enable them in the project or organisation IAM & Admin → Audit Logs UI, or via the google_project_iam_audit_config Terraform resource.

  • DATA_READS: records who read a resource (e.g., storage.objects.get, bigquery.tables.getData).
  • DATA_WRITES: records who wrote or modified a resource.
  • ADMIN_READS: records metadata operations (e.g., listing objects). Often voluminous — enable selectively to avoid log storage costs.

A common audit failure mode: a team enables Data Access logging at the project level, but the audit window began before they enabled it. There is no retroactive log generation. The fix is enabling at the organisation level from day one, not after an auditor requests evidence.

Access Transparency Logs

Access Transparency logs are distinct from audit logs — they record when Google staff access your content for operational reasons (support, incident investigation). These logs appear in Cloud Logging as cloudaudit.googleapis.com/access_transparency entries and include a justification code explaining why access occurred. They are available on projects with Gold or Platinum support and in select GCP services. For regulated industries, Access Transparency provides assurance that even Google's operational access is documented and reviewable.

Access Transparency ≠ Cloud Audit Logs. Cloud Audit Logs record your users' and services' actions on GCP resources. Access Transparency logs record Google employees' actions on your data. A question asking "how do you know if Google staff accessed your data?" points to Access Transparency. A question asking "how do you know if your service account accessed a Cloud Storage bucket?" points to Data Access audit logs.
"The auditor asked for seven years of logs. The engineer checked Cloud Logging. It had 30 days. The log sink to Cloud Storage had been on the backlog since Q1 2019. The auditor had a very good poker face." 📋😬

Automating Evidence Collection with Cloud Scheduler

Manual evidence gathering at audit time is a compliance antipattern — it's slow, inconsistent, and provides only a point-in-time snapshot rather than a continuous record. Automate the collection pipeline:

  • Cloud Scheduler → Cloud Functions → Cloud Asset Inventory export: schedule a daily CAI export to a Cloud Storage bucket. Each export file is a timestamped snapshot of every resource and IAM policy in the org — an instant audit artefact.
  • Cloud Scheduler → IAM Recommender API: export IAM Recommender findings daily to BigQuery. Track over time whether granted permissions are being actioned — evidence of ongoing access review.
  • Log sink aggregation: configure an aggregated log sink at the org level (not per-project) to capture audit logs from all current and future projects automatically. Projects created after the sink was set up are included without any additional configuration.
  • SCC findings to BigQuery: SCC can stream all findings to BigQuery continuously. Query the findings table to produce compliance reports, track mean-time-to-remediate, and demonstrate trend improvement to auditors.

⚠️ Per-Project Log Sinks Miss New Projects

A common mistake is configuring log sinks at the project level — this means every new project is a gap in your audit trail until someone remembers to add the sink. Always configure an aggregated log sink at the organisation or folder level with the includeChildren: true flag. This captures logs from all current and future child resources automatically — no per-project ops toil, no audit gaps.

Section 5.8 Quick Reference

  • Data Access audit logs: explicitly enable per-service — off by default (BigQuery is the exception). Without these, you cannot answer "who read this data?"
  • Admin Activity logs: always enabled, records all resource create/modify/delete API calls.
  • _Required log bucket: 400-day retention, non-configurable. Covers Admin Activity and System Event logs.
  • _Default log bucket: 30-day default retention. Extend in Cloud Logging, or sink to Cloud Storage for long-term archival.
  • 7-year retention: log sink → Cloud Storage → Bucket Lock with 7-year retention policy. Cheapest and most durable long-term archival path.
  • Cloud Asset Inventory: point-in-time and historical IAM policy snapshots. The go-to tool for "who had access when" evidence.
  • Org-level aggregated log sink: captures logs from all current and future projects automatically — always prefer this over per-project sinks for compliance.
  • Access Transparency: records Google staff access to your data — distinct from user audit logs.

🧠 Quick-Fire Warm-Up Quiz!

Retrieve before you review. No peeking!

Q1: A regulated workload must run only in EU regions with controls to support a named compliance regime. Which service?

Assured Workloads. It enforces data residency, personnel, and control constraints for compliance regimes.

Q2: You must explicitly approve any Google support access to your data before it happens. Which feature?

Access Approval (paired with Access Transparency). It requires explicit customer approval and logs Google access.

Q3: You only need a log showing when Google staff accessed your content during support, with no approval workflow. Which feature?

Access Transparency. It records Google administrative access to your content as logs, without gating it.

Q4: Prevent developers from creating any resource outside approved regions, without buying a full compliance program. Which control?

The resource-location organization policy (constraints/gcp.resourceLocations). It restricts where resources can be created and inherits across the hierarchy.

Q5: A service account gets a 403 on a BigQuery dataset. You need to know exactly why right now. Which Policy Intelligence tool do you reach for?

Policy Troubleshooter. It explains a specific access denial at a point in time — walking through IAM bindings and org policies to show exactly what blocked the request. Policy Analyzer would tell you who has access broadly, not why this request was denied.

Q6: Your team wants to verify that removing the roles/bigquery.dataViewer binding from an external group won't break any active pipelines before committing the change. Which tool?

Policy Simulator. It replays recent access logs against the proposed policy change to show which accesses would succeed or fail — in a read-only, hypothetical mode. The change is not applied.

Q7: Security Command Center finds a public GCS bucket. Nothing else happens. What's missing if you want the bucket fixed automatically within minutes?

The Pub/Sub → Cloud Function remediation pipeline. SHA creates the finding, but SCC does not auto-remediate. You must configure SCC to export findings to a Pub/Sub topic, with a Cloud Function subscribed to that topic that calls the Storage API to remove the allUsers binding.

Question 1: A government customer's regulation requires that any Google support engineer who touches their data must be located in-country, that data stays in approved regions, and that only an approved service set is used. Which offering meets all three at once?

  • A) Access Approval combined with the resource-location organization policy.
  • B) Assured Workloads configured for the relevant compliance regime.
  • C) A VPC Service Controls perimeter plus CMEK on all data services.
  • D) Access Transparency logs reviewed alongside Security Command Center findings.

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct: Assured Workloads is the single offering that bundles personnel controls (support-staff location), data residency, and a restricted approved-service set for a chosen compliance regime.

Why the others are wrong:
A: This pair covers approval and residency but not the support-personnel location requirement.
C: VPC-SC and CMEK protect data exfiltration and keys but say nothing about where support staff are located or residency enforcement.
D: These provide visibility into access and posture, not enforcement of residency, personnel, or service constraints.

Question 2: An auditor for a PCI assessment asks you to demonstrate that your in-scope projects are continuously checked against an industry benchmark and to produce evidence of the findings. Which approach gives this with the least custom build?

  • A) Export all audit logs to BigQuery and write SQL that re-implements the benchmark checks.
  • B) Schedule a third-party scanner against each project and collate its reports manually.
  • C) Use the Security Command Center compliance dashboard mapped to the relevant benchmark.
  • D) Enable Access Transparency and present the Google-access logs as compliance evidence.

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct: Security Command Center maps Security Health Analytics findings to industry benchmarks such as PCI DSS and CIS out of the box, providing continuous checks and exportable evidence with minimal build.

Why the others are wrong:
A: Re-implementing benchmark logic in SQL is exactly the heavy custom build the question asks you to avoid.
B: Manual collation of third-party reports is neither continuous nor low-effort.
D: Access Transparency logs Google access; it's unrelated to benchmarking your configuration against PCI.

Question 3: A data-sovereignty rule says certain workloads must never have resources created outside two approved European regions, and the restriction must hold across all current and future projects without depending on per-project setup. What enforces this?

  • A) Setting the resource-location organization policy at the org node listing the two approved regions.
  • B) A hierarchical firewall policy permitting only traffic to and from the two regions.
  • C) Tagging resources by region and auditing the tags with a scheduled report.
  • D) A VPC Service Controls perimeter restricted to subnets in the two approved regions.

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct: The gcp.resourceLocations org policy set at the org node inherits to all current and future projects and blocks resource creation outside the listed regions — preventive, not detective.

Why the others are wrong:
B: Firewall policies govern network traffic, not where resources may be created.
C: Tagging and reporting only detects violations after the fact and relies on per-resource discipline.
D: VPC-SC limits API data movement across a perimeter; it doesn't constrain the regions in which resources are provisioned.
