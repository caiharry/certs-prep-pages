Section 1A: Cloud Database Infrastructure & Connections

Capacity sizing, High Availability, Disaster Recovery, and unlocking hybrid network routing secrets without losing your mind!

Section 1 Part A 🎯

1.1 Sizing Up the Beast — Capacity & Sizing

Right then! Let's kick off with a classic exam trap: guessing your database size. Don't do it! Google doesn't like guesswork, and neither does your bank balance. To pass this exam, you need cold, hard evidence!

📈 Sizing from Evidence — No Guessing Allowed!

When you are asked to size compute and storage for a migration, don't just close your eyes and point at a random virtual machine. You need to gather actual performance metrics over a representative window — we're talking weeks or months, not just a cheeky five-minute check during lunchtime traffic! Here's your checklist of things to hunt down:

  • Peak vs. Average CPU & Memory: Don't just look at averages or your database will crash during peak sales! Always size for the peak workload, but keep an eye on averages to avoid burning cash.
  • Peak IOPS & Throughput: Crucial for choosing the right disk tier. Mess this up and your queries will be stuck in a massive traffic jam!
  • Active & Max Connection Counts: Every connection gobbles up memory. If you run out of connection capacity, your app will get the cold shoulder!
  • Storage Growth Rate: Databases have a habit of growing. You need to predict when you'll run out of disk space before it actually happens!
Watch out for the Over-Provisioned Instance Recommender! If the exam scenario mentions you have months of historical metrics and you want to save money, don't calculate everything by hand. Reach for this handy built-in tool! It acts like a cheeky little helper that scans your metrics and flags oversized instances so you can scale them down automatically.

💾 Storage Showdown — HDD vs. SSD?

This is a quick-fire examiner favorite. Get this wrong and your database will run slower than a snail wearing lead boots:

  • SSD (Solid State Drives): Your absolute go-to for OLTP (Transactional) workloads. If you need low-latency and high IOPS, SSD is your best friend! 🚀
  • HDD (Hard Disk Drives): Only for massive, freezing-cold archive/backup data where latency doesn't matter and you are being extremely tight-fisted with the budget! 🥶
🧠 Crucial Memory Tip: The Cloud SQL Disk Trap!

Write this on your hand: On Cloud SQL, IOPS and throughput scale directly with disk size! If you provision a tiny 10 GB disk, your IOPS will be absolutely pathetic. To get more speed, you often have to buy more storage than you actually need, just to boost the IOPS limit! Sneaky, eh?

"Why did the database administrator get kicked out of the playground? Because they couldn't handle the spin on the HDD!" 🌀

1.2 Keeping It Alive — HA vs. DR!

Now pay attention! There is a massive difference between surviving a minor server hiccup and surviving a full-blown meteor strike on a data centre. Examiners love to see if you can tell them apart!

Footprint Availability SLA What it survives Failover Process
Zonal (Single instance) No HA SLA (Cheap!) Does not survive a zone outage 💀 Manual recreation from backup (Painfully slow!)
Regional HA (Cloud SQL/AlloyDB) Cloud SQL Enterprise: 99.95%
Cloud SQL Enterprise Plus: 99.99%
AlloyDB HA: usually 99.99%		 Survives a Zone outage 🏢 Automatic failover to synchronous standby in Zone B
Multi-Regional (Cloud Spanner) 99.999% (Five Nines!) 🌟 Survives an entire Region outage 🌍 Platform-managed replication and routing; still design app for retries!

⚠️ Warning: High Availability is NOT Disaster Recovery!

Let's get this straight: HA is for local glitches; DR is for regional apocalypses!

If you enable High Availability (HA) on Cloud SQL or AlloyDB, Google spins up a synchronous standby replica in a different zone in the same region. If Zone A goes dark, it fails over automatically to Zone B in seconds. Brilliant! 🌟

But what if a massive flood takes out the entire region? Since Zone B is in the same region, your standby is underwater too! 🌊

For Disaster Recovery (DR), you need a Cross-Region Read Replica. If the main region dies, you must manually promote that replica to be your new primary instance.

The Sneaky Exam Catch: Cross-region replication is asynchronous (meaning it's always a tiny bit behind the primary). If the primary region gets zapped, any writes that hadn't reached the cross-region replica yet are lost forever! Point-In-Time Recovery (PITR) is great for undoing user mistakes, but it can't magically restore writes that never arrived at the DR site!

⚙️ Dodging the Maintenance Mallet!

Even fully-managed databases need upgrades and security patches. Google will perform these during a designated maintenance window. If you don't plan for this, your database will restart when your users are busiest, causing total chaos! Here is how to keep things smooth:

  1. Pick a low-traffic window: Don't run updates at 9 AM on Monday! Schedule them for 3 AM on a Sunday instead. 😴
  2. Set the Maintenance Order: Configure your dev databases to update days before production (e.g., "preview" vs. "production" settings). This way, if an update breaks something, it breaks dev first, not your production checkout!
  3. App-Side Resilience: Always use a connection pooler and write exponential backoff retries into your application code. During the brief failover/restart blip (usually under 60 seconds), your app shouldn't panic and crash — it will just patiently try again!

1.3 Lock It Down — How Applications Connect & Advanced Networking

Security is absolute king. Leaving your database open to the public internet is like leaving your front door wide open with a giant neon sign saying "Free Jewellery Inside!" 🏠💍

🔒 Private IPs only: PSA vs. PSC

To keep the bad guys out, always use Private IP addresses for your databases. Google manages your databases in a special network, so you need to bridge your VPC to theirs. Here are the two ways to do it:

  • Private Services Access (PSA): The classic method. It uses a VPC Peering connection over a reserved IP range. It works perfectly, but VPC peerings are notoriously stubborn and do not transitive-route easily. If you have complex hub-and-spoke networks, it can become a real headache! 🤯
  • Private Service Connect (PSC): The modern, shiny way! Instead of peering networks, it places a local endpoint (Private IP) directly inside your consumer VPC. It's incredibly secure, flexible, and transitive-routing is a breeze! 🌟

Exam Tip: Avoid public IPs with "authorized networks" lists like the plague. If the examiners ask for the most secure, Google-recommended approach, it's always Private IP via PSA or PSC!

🔥 Advanced Network Route Traps: PSA vs. PSC under the Hood!

Examiners love testing your understanding of hybrid routing. If your database is fully private (no public IP), how do developers or apps located on-premises (connected via Cloud VPN or Interconnect) reach it? Let's break down the rules of the road:

🚨 The Non-Transitive Peering Wall (PSA Routing Trap)

If you connect to your database using Private Services Access (PSA), Google sets up a VPC Peering connection between your consumer VPC and a Google-managed tenant VPC. Here is the giant, neon-flashing catch:

VPC Peering is non-transitive! This means traffic cannot hop from one peered network to another. So, your on-premises network (Network 1) can talk to your VPC (Network 2) via VPN/Interconnect, but it cannot automatically hop through your VPC peering connection to talk to the Google-managed tenant VPC (Network 3)! Traffic simply hits a physical wall and dies. 🧱

🛠️ How to smash the wall (The PSA Fix):

  1. Go to your VPC Network Peering connection config (specifically the peering connection named servicenetworking-googleapis-com).
  2. Update it to explicitly "Export Custom Routes" so your VPC tells the Google-managed VPC about your on-premises subnet ranges. (You must also ensure Google-managed network is configured to import them).
  3. Go to your Cloud Router that manages your VPN/Interconnect. Add a Custom Route Advertisement to advertise the allocated IP range of your PSA database (e.g., 10.0.0.0/24) back to your on-premises routers via BGP. This tells your on-premises network how to reach the database! 📡

✨ The PSC Native Routing Advantage

Now contrast this with Private Service Connect (PSC). Because PSC creates a local endpoint (Private IP) directly inside one of your VPC subnets, it is treated like a standard local VM IP inside your network. There is no non-transitive peering relationship involved!

Because it's a local subnet IP, your on-premises networks can route traffic to it natively over VPN or Interconnect without any peering updates! All you need to do is advertise the PSC endpoint IP or its subnet through your standard Cloud Router BGP configuration. It's clean, simple, and avoids all routing head-scratchers. 🌟

🌍 Global Dynamic Routing & SOCKS5 Tunneling Secrets

Here are two more advanced connectivity tricks that might save your bacon under pressure:

  • Global Dynamic Routing for Cross-Region Access: By default, if your VPC dynamic routing mode is set to Regional, resources in Region A (like a VM or a Serverless VPC Access connector for Cloud Run) cannot route traffic to a private database in Region B. To allow cross-region routing over VPN/Interconnect or between subnets, you must set your VPC's dynamic routing mode to Global.
  • Auth Proxy with SOCKS5 Proxy Chaining: What if a developer is working from home and their machine has no direct network reachability to your VPC? The Cloud SQL Auth Proxy can chain through a SOCKS5 proxy! You set up a tiny proxy VM inside your VPC, and run the Auth Proxy on the developer's laptop with the ALL_PROXY environment variable pointing to that VM. It safely tunnels the encrypted SQL traffic through! 
    ALL_PROXY=socks5://my-proxy-bastion:8000 cloud-sql-proxy my-instance
  • VPC Service Controls (VPC-SC): If you are dealing with ultra-secure government or banking data, you will place your databases inside a VPC Service Controls perimeter. This isolates your database APIs from the public internet, completely preventing data exfiltration (where an attacker tries to copy or export your tables to an external, unauthorized GCP project).
Cloud SQL Auth Proxy is your golden ticket! If your apps on GKE or Compute Engine need to connect securely, use the Cloud SQL Auth Proxy (or language-specific Cloud SQL Connectors). It automatically handles SSL/TLS encryption and authorizes connections using IAM Roles! That means zero SQL passwords or SSL keys for your developers to rotate, leak, or lose! Absolute bliss!

Make sure you memorize these three high-yield operational details for the exam:

  • IAM Client Authentication: The Auth Proxy uses GCP IAM service accounts or user identities to establish the secure tunnel. The identity running the proxy must have the Cloud SQL Client (roles/cloudsql.client) IAM role on the database instance. Note: The proxy only validates the network tunnel; you still log into the actual database using database-level users/passwords (or IAM database users) inside that secure tunnel.
  • GKE Sidecar Pattern: On Google Kubernetes Engine (GKE), deploy the proxy as a Sidecar container inside the same Kubernetes Pod as your application. Because containers in the same Pod share the localhost network namespace, your app can simply connect to 127.0.0.1:5432 (Postgres) or 127.0.0.1:3306 (MySQL) to connect securely and instantly!
  • Private-IP Routing Flag: You do NOT need a public IP to use the Auth Proxy! By passing the -private-ip flag (or ip_type = "PRIVATE" in modern connectors), you force the proxy to route connections strictly over Private Services Access (PSA) or Private Service Connect (PSC). This keeps your data completely off the public internet! 🔒

💧 Managed Connection Pooling — Stop the Flood!

Every time an app establishes a new database connection, your DB has to burn precious CPU and RAM to set it up. If you have hundreds of serverless containers (like Cloud Run) spinning up and tearing down, your database will quickly run out of breath and collapse! 😵

To prevent this, use Managed Connection Pooling (like the built-in pooler in Cloud SQL or AlloyDB). It keeps a steady pool of "warm" connections open, sharing them out to incoming requests.

🕵️ The Connection Dynamic Duo:

Don't confuse the Auth Proxy with the Connection Pooler. They have very different day jobs:

  • The Auth Proxy is the Bouncer at the door — checking your IAM badges and encrypting the traffic. 👮
  • The Connection Pooler is the Cloakroom Attendant inside — organizing, saving space, and recycling connections so the room doesn't get overcrowded! 🧥

🧠 Quick-Fire Warm-Up Quiz!

Test your recall before diving into the proper practice exam. No peeking!

Q1: True or False: Cloud SQL High Availability (HA) protects against a full regional failure.

False! Cloud SQL HA is strictly zonal (synchronous standby in another zone of the same region). For regional protection, you need a Cross-Region Read Replica.

Q2: What tool should you use to check if you have over-allocated resource sizes on database instances over the last month?

Over-Provisioned Instance Recommender! It automatically scans metrics and gives sizing advice, saving you from manual math.

Q3: Why can't an on-premises network natively talk to a PSA-provisioned Cloud SQL database over a VPN connection?

Because VPC Peering is non-transitive! The traffic from on-premises cannot automatically route across the peered network boundary. You must configure the peering connection to "Export Custom Routes" and configure Cloud Router to advertise the database range.

Q4: To allow resources in Region A to route private traffic to a private database located in Region B, what VPC configuration is required?

You must set the VPC's dynamic routing mode to Global. If left as Regional, cross-region subnet routes will not propagate dynamically!

📝 Section 1A Practice Exam Questions

Let's see if you can handle these actual exam-style infrastructure and networking brain busters!

Question 1: You are designing HA and DR for a critical MySQL database on Cloud SQL. The business wants near-zero RPO and sub-minute RTO for zonal failures, plus a workable regional DR path with the lowest practical data loss. How should you configure this?

  • A) Configure a zonal Cloud SQL instance with daily automated backups shipped to a multi-regional Cloud Storage bucket, and configure a Cloud Run function to automatically deploy a replica upon zonal failure.
  • B) Enable High Availability (Regional HA), configure a cross-region read replica, enable PITR, and monitor replica lag.
  • C) Deploy a multi-regional Cloud SQL instance spanning three separate regions, and use Cloud DNS routing policies to split the write load among active instances.
  • D) Provision two zonal Cloud SQL instances in separate regions, and write a custom Java-based middleware application with dynamic retry policies to perform synchronous dual-writes on both instances.

Correct Answer: B

Why Option B is correct: Regional HA gives the zonal-failure behaviour: automatic failover to a synchronous standby in the same region. A cross-region read replica gives the regional DR target, PITR protects against logical mistakes, and replica-lag monitoring tells you the real regional RPO risk.

  • A: Daily backups are useful, but they are too slow and too lossy for the stated zonal HA requirement.
  • C: Cloud SQL does not provide a single multi-region primary instance in the Spanner sense.
  • D: DIY dual writes are a consistency goblin factory. Avoid unless the question explicitly demands custom application-level replication.

Question 2: Your company has adopted a complex hub-and-spoke VPC network topology on Google Cloud. You need to connect multiple consumer spoke VPC networks to a central managed Cloud SQL PostgreSQL database instance. The company's security policy strictly forbids direct VPC peering between the spoke networks, and the network team warns that transitive routing over VPC peering is not supported. How should you design this private database connectivity?

  • A) Create a Private Services Access (PSA) connection in the Hub VPC, configure dynamic BGP route leaking across all spokes, and rely on standard transitive peering to route spoke traffic to Cloud SQL.
  • B) Deploy Private Service Connect (PSC) in the Cloud SQL producer network, and create a local PSC endpoint (Private IP) directly in each consumer spoke VPC.
  • C) Configure a public IP address on the Cloud SQL instance, lock it down with a hierarchical VPC firewall rule list, and route spoke traffic through public NAT gateways.
  • D) Use Cloud VPN to establish direct IPSec tunnels between the Cloud SQL tenant project VPC network and each independent consumer spoke VPC subnet.

Correct Answer: B

Why Option B is correct: Private Service Connect (PSC) is the modern, flexible way to bridge networks! Unlike PSA (which relies on VPC Peering), PSC uses local endpoints (Private IPs) in each consumer spoke VPC directly. This completely bypasses the limitations of non-transitive VPC Peering routing and allows multi-tenant spoke networks to safely access a shared central database service without peering with each other or the hub. 🌟

Why the others are wrong:

  • A: Private Services Access (PSA) uses VPC Peering, which does not support transitive routing. Spoke VPCs would not be able to reach Cloud SQL via the Hub peering connection.
  • C: Public IPs with Authorized Networks are less secure and violate the private-network design constraint.
  • D: Cloud VPN is a complex, high-overhead way to bridge networks, and Google Cloud SQL does not allow you to terminate customer VPN tunnels inside its managed tenant project network.

Question 3: You are deploying a high-volume microservices application on Google Kubernetes Engine (GKE) that must connect securely to a private Cloud SQL PostgreSQL instance over Private IP. The security team strictly prohibits the use of static database passwords or long-lived SSL certificates in your Kubernetes secret configurations. What is the recommended architecture and deployment pattern?

  • A) Enable a public IP address on the Cloud SQL instance, authorize your GKE cluster's dynamic external NAT IP pool in the firewall, and configure your microservices to connect using base64 encoded credentials.
  • B) Deploy the Cloud SQL Auth Proxy as a sidecar container in your application Pods. Configure GKE Workload Identity to bind your Kubernetes service account to a GCP IAM service account with the Cloud SQL Client (roles/cloudsql.client) role. Configure the proxy to connect over Private IP using the -private-ip flag, and configure your app to connect to localhost.
  • C) Install the Cloud SQL Auth Proxy as a dynamic DaemonSet across all GKE nodes, and expose its port via an external GKE ingress controller configured with long-lived SSL certificates.
  • D) Deploy your Cloud SQL instance with Private Services Access (PSA) enabled, generate self-signed SSL client certificates, and mount them as persistent secret volumes inside your application container specifications.

Correct Answer: B

Why Option B is correct: This is the absolute gold standard for GKE-to-Cloud SQL security!

  • Workload Identity: Eliminates static credentials. The application pod assumes a secure IAM role dynamically.
  • Sidecar Pattern: Running the proxy inside the same Pod allows the application to communicate with it securely over localhost (127.0.0.1) with zero complex network routing.
  • -private-ip Flag: Forces the proxy to route connections over your private network, ensuring database traffic never touches the public internet. 🔒

Why the others are wrong:

  • A: Public IPs and static passwords violate both the private IP mandate and the static credential prohibition.
  • C: Running the proxy as a cluster-wide DaemonSet or service introduces extra network routing hops, security exposure, and does not secure Pod-to-Node traffic.
  • D: Self-signed certs require manual management, Rotation overhead, and public subnets violate network requirements.

Question 4: Your corporate network is connected to Google Cloud via Partner Interconnect. Developers working on-premises need to connect directly to a private Cloud SQL instance that has been provisioned via Private Services Access (PSA). Although they can ping VMs inside the consumer VPC, they cannot reach the database's internal IP address. Subnet routing and local firewall rules allow traffic. What is the root cause and recommended solution?

  • A) Cloud SQL does not natively support private connections originating from on-premises networks; configure a public IP on Cloud SQL and manage connections via an IPsec VPN tunnel gateway.
  • B) VPC Peering is non-transitive. Update the VPC peering connection configuration to "Export Custom Routes", configure PSA to import custom routes, and add a custom route advertisement for the allocated Private Services Access IP range on the VPC's Cloud Router BGP configuration.
  • C) The dynamic routing mode of the central VPC network is set to Global. Change the routing mode to Regional and recreate the dynamic Cloud Router configuration to force subnet advertisement convergence.
  • D) Deploy a secondary passive Cloud SQL instance inside on-premises virtualized infrastructure, and set up MySQL native replication over a dedicated physical Interconnect port.

Correct Answer: B

Why Option B is correct: This is a massive, high-yield networking trap! Under the hood, Private Services Access (PSA) uses VPC Peering. Because VPC Peering is non-transitive, traffic coming from on-premises via VPN or Interconnect cannot automatically cross the peered boundary into the Google-managed tenant network where Cloud SQL sits. To solve this:

  1. You must explicitly tell the peering to Export Custom Routes so on-premises routes known to your VPC are shared across the peering link.
  2. You must configure your Cloud Router (which runs the BGP session with your on-premises routers) to add a Custom Route Advertisement that advertises the database's private allocated CIDR block (not your local subnets, but the range allocated to Google services) back to your on-premises routers. Without this BGP advertisement, on-premises has no idea how to route packets to the database's IP address!

Why the others are wrong:

  • A: Cloud SQL fully supports on-premises private connectivity, provided the routing is correctly configured. Enabling public IP violates standard security best practices.
  • C: Setting routing to regional actually prevents cross-region routing! Global routing is needed if resources are cross-region. It would not fix the non-transitive peering block.
  • D: Cloud SQL does not support running on-premises instances inside standard master-slave managed replication setups, and this is completely over-engineered.
