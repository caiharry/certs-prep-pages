Section 5: Supporting Compliance

Map regulatory and industry requirements onto Google Cloud controls (~11%). The smallest slice on the paper, and mostly common sense once you know the shared responsibility model — easy marks if you don't fumble three look-alike services.

Section 5 🟩 ~11%

Only one in nine questions lives here, so don't over-invest — but don't skip it either, because the marks are cheap. The whole domain boils down to: know who's responsible for what, keep data in the right region, control Google's own access, and prove compliance with evidence. The one trap is three services that sound identical (Transparency, Approval, Assured Workloads) but do very different jobs. 📜✅

5.1 The Shared Responsibility Model

Compliance starts with knowing where Google's job ends and yours begins. Google secures the infrastructure (hardware, the global network, the hypervisor, physical data centres). You secure what you put on it: identity, IAM configuration, your data, and your application code. The split shifts as you move up the stack — with serverless products Google takes on more, but your data and access decisions are always yours.

🤝 "Shared Fate", Not Just Shared Responsibility

Google's modern framing is shared fate: Google doesn't just draw a line and walk away — it provides secure-by-default blueprints, Assured Workloads, and risk-protection programs to help you hold up your side. But the accountability for configuration and data never transfers.

5.2 The Three Look-Alikes: Transparency vs Approval vs Assured Workloads

This is the only genuinely tricky bit of the domain. All three concern regulated estates and Google's access to your data — but they sit at different strengths.

Control What It Does Strength
Access Transparency Logs when Google staff access your content (e.g. during a support case). Visibility only — you SEE it, after the fact.
Access Approval Google must request, and you must explicitly approve, before that access happens. A GATE — you approve it first.
Assured Workloads A compliance-boundary folder enforcing data residency, personnel controls, and an approved-service set. A BOUNDARY — broad enforcement for FedRAMP, CJIS, EU sovereignty, etc.

💡 SEE / GATE / BOUND

Transparency lets you SEE (a passive log). Approval is the GATE (you say yes first). Assured Workloads draws the BOUNDary (residency + personnel + services). Match the verb in the stem to the right control.

"We need a log of Google access" = Access Transparency. "We must approve it first" = Access Approval. Don't confuse the passive log with the active gate. And if the stem adds "support staff must be in-country" or "only approved services / specific region," that personnel-and-residency bundle is Assured Workloads — the others can't enforce personnel location.

5.3 Enforcing Data Residency

Two tools enforce where data and resources live, and you should know when each fits:

Tool What It Enforces Use When...
Resource-location org policy (gcp.resourceLocations) Blocks resource creation outside approved regions; inherits to all current & future projects. You just need a residency guardrail without a full compliance program.
Assured Workloads Residency plus personnel controls, approved services, and regime alignment. A named regime (FedRAMP, CJIS, EU sovereignty) demands the full bundle.
Residency is preventive, not detective. If the requirement is "must never be created outside region X, across all current and future projects, with no per-project setup," the answer is the resource-location org policy at the org node — not tagging-and-auditing (detective, after the fact) and not a firewall policy (governs traffic, not where resources are provisioned).

5.4 Demonstrating Compliance With Evidence

Mapping a requirement (SOC 2, PCI DSS, ISO 27001, data sovereignty) onto Google Cloud means translating it into concrete controls and being able to prove them:

  • Security Command Center compliance dashboard — maps Security Health Analytics findings to benchmarks like CIS, PCI DSS, and NIST out of the box. Continuous checks, exportable evidence, minimal build.
  • Cloud Audit Logs — the who-did-what record auditors ask for.
  • CMEK — demonstrable key control for data-at-rest requirements.
  • Scoping — decide which projects are in scope for each framework and isolate them with the resource hierarchy and VPC Service Controls, so the audit surface stays small.

💡 Don't Re-Build the Benchmark

If the stem says "continuously checked against an industry benchmark, with the least custom build," the answer is the SCC compliance dashboard — never "export logs to BigQuery and write SQL to re-implement PCI." Re-implementing the benchmark by hand is exactly the wrong answer.

"The auditor asked for evidence of continuous compliance. I showed them the SCC dashboard. They asked for a second espresso instead — we were done early." ☕📊

🧠 Quick-Fire Warm-Up Quiz!

Retrieve before you review. No peeking!

Q1: A regulated workload must run only in EU regions with controls to support a named compliance regime. Which service?

Assured Workloads. It enforces data residency, personnel, and control constraints for compliance regimes.

Q2: You must explicitly approve any Google support access to your data before it happens. Which feature?

Access Approval (paired with Access Transparency). It requires explicit customer approval and logs Google access.

Q3: You only need a log showing when Google staff accessed your content during support, with no approval workflow. Which feature?

Access Transparency. It records Google administrative access to your content as logs, without gating it.

Q4: Prevent developers from creating any resource outside approved regions, without buying a full compliance program. Which control?

The resource-location organization policy (constraints/gcp.resourceLocations). It restricts where resources can be created and inherits across the hierarchy.

Question 1: A government customer's regulation requires that any Google support engineer who touches their data must be located in-country, that data stays in approved regions, and that only an approved service set is used. Which offering meets all three at once?

  • A) Access Approval combined with the resource-location organization policy.
  • B) Assured Workloads configured for the relevant compliance regime.
  • C) A VPC Service Controls perimeter plus CMEK on all data services.
  • D) Access Transparency logs reviewed alongside Security Command Center findings.

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct: Assured Workloads is the single offering that bundles personnel controls (support-staff location), data residency, and a restricted approved-service set for a chosen compliance regime.

Why the others are wrong:
A: This pair covers approval and residency but not the support-personnel location requirement.
C: VPC-SC and CMEK protect data exfiltration and keys but say nothing about where support staff are located or residency enforcement.
D: These provide visibility into access and posture, not enforcement of residency, personnel, or service constraints.

Question 2: An auditor for a PCI assessment asks you to demonstrate that your in-scope projects are continuously checked against an industry benchmark and to produce evidence of the findings. Which approach gives this with the least custom build?

  • A) Export all audit logs to BigQuery and write SQL that re-implements the benchmark checks.
  • B) Schedule a third-party scanner against each project and collate its reports manually.
  • C) Use the Security Command Center compliance dashboard mapped to the relevant benchmark.
  • D) Enable Access Transparency and present the Google-access logs as compliance evidence.

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct: Security Command Center maps Security Health Analytics findings to industry benchmarks such as PCI DSS and CIS out of the box, providing continuous checks and exportable evidence with minimal build.

Why the others are wrong:
A: Re-implementing benchmark logic in SQL is exactly the heavy custom build the question asks you to avoid.
B: Manual collation of third-party reports is neither continuous nor low-effort.
D: Access Transparency logs Google access; it's unrelated to benchmarking your configuration against PCI.

Question 3: A data-sovereignty rule says certain workloads must never have resources created outside two approved European regions, and the restriction must hold across all current and future projects without depending on per-project setup. What enforces this?

  • A) Setting the resource-location organization policy at the org node listing the two approved regions.
  • B) A hierarchical firewall policy permitting only traffic to and from the two regions.
  • C) Tagging resources by region and auditing the tags with a scheduled report.
  • D) A VPC Service Controls perimeter restricted to subnets in the two approved regions.

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct: The gcp.resourceLocations org policy set at the org node inherits to all current and future projects and blocks resource creation outside the listed regions — preventive, not detective.

Why the others are wrong:
B: Firewall policies govern network traffic, not where resources may be created.
C: Tagging and reporting only detects violations after the fact and relies on per-resource discipline.
D: VPC-SC limits API data movement across a perimeter; it doesn't constrain the regions in which resources are provisioned.
